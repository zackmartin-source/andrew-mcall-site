[2] The Question That Changes Everything

[3] "Most are subjective toward themselves and objective toward all others,

[4] frightfully sometimes. But the task is precisely to be objective toward

[5] oneself and subjective toward all others."

[6] Soren Kierkegaard

[7] There is a question buried in the opening chapters of Genesis that most readers walk past without stopping. It does not announce itself. It does not interrupt the narrative with a footnote or demand that the reader pause. It sits in plain sight, wrapped inside a simple command, in a garden that no longer exists in any geography we can visit. And yet it may be the most consequential question ever put before the human race.

[8] The question is this: why was the tree of the knowledge of good and evil forbidden?

[9] Not the serpent. Not the act of disobedience itself. Not even the fruit, which most artistic traditions have imagined as an apple with no scriptural basis whatsoever. The question behind all of those details is the one this book is written to pursue. Because the answer to that question does not merely explain a moment in Eden. It explains the entire shape of human history. It reframes the covenants. It reframes the prophets. It reframes the Incarnation. And it reframes the life of every person who has ever tried to navigate the world while carrying questions that only the Source of all knowledge can answer.

[10] This is not a book about the Fall in the way most books about the Fall are written. It is not primarily a book about sin and its consequences, though sin and consequences will appear in every chapter. It is a book about a boundary. About a relationship. About the difference between two kinds of knowledge. And about a God who placed one tree in the middle of a garden because He wanted His children to get everything they needed from Him.

[11] Kierkegaard at the Gate of Eden

[12] Soren Kierkegaard was not thinking about the garden of Eden when he wrote the words that open this chapter. He was writing about the fundamental problem of human self-knowledge. He was a Danish philosopher and theologian working in the first half of the nineteenth century, and he was trying to describe the peculiar blindness that afflicts almost every human being who has ever lived: the tendency to extend enormous generosity to oneself while remaining harshly critical of everyone else.

[13] Most people, Kierkegaard observed, are subjective toward themselves. They interpret their own motives charitably. They find ways to explain their failures that shift the weight of responsibility somewhere else. They give themselves the benefit of every doubt that they would never extend to another person. And they are correspondingly objective toward others, meaning they evaluate other people with a critical distance and analytical precision that they never turn on themselves.

[14] The task, Kierkegaard said, is to reverse this. To be objective toward oneself. To be willing to look honestly, without the protection of self-flattery, at who you actually are, where you are actually getting your knowledge, and what you are actually doing with it. And to be subjective toward others, meaning open, receptive, and willing to see what God might be revealing through the people, events, and circumstances that surround you.

[15] This reversal is not a psychological exercise. It is a theological one. And it lands with remarkable precision on the oldest story in Scripture, because the tree of the knowledge of good and evil was forbidden for exactly the reason Kierkegaard was trying to describe. Humanity was not meant to be the subject of its own knowing. God was meant to be the Source. The tree represented the moment when humanity chose to become the center of its own epistemological universe, the arbiter of what is good and what is evil, the generator of a knowledge that was always meant to flow from somewhere else.

[16] The book of Proverbs, centuries before Kierkegaard, named this same tendency with a directness that still cuts: There is a way that seems right to a man, but its end is the way of death (Proverbs 14:12, ESV). The way that seems right. The subjective assessment. The knowledge generated from inside the self rather than received from above. This is what the tree offered. And this is what God was protecting His children from when He drew the boundary.

[17] And out of the ground made the LORD God to grow every tree that is pleasant to the sight, and good for food; the tree of life also in the midst of the garden, and the tree of knowledge of good and evil.

[18] Genesis 2:9 (KJV)

[19] Read that verse carefully, and notice what it does not say. It does not say the tree of the knowledge of good and evil was dangerous in appearance. It does not say it was set apart by some visual warning, some divine sign that would have marked it as off-limits from a distance. It was pleasant to the sight and good for food. In the later account of Eve's deliberation, we are told she saw that the tree was good for food, and that it was a delight to the eyes, and that the tree was to be desired to make one wise (Genesis 3:6, ESV). The tree was appealing by every measure that the human senses could apply to it.

[20] This is important. The tree was not forbidden because it was obviously bad. It was forbidden because the knowledge it offered was not the knowledge God wanted His children to have, obtained in the way He wanted them to obtain it. The problem was never the tree itself. The problem was the source the tree represented: self-generated, independently acquired, boundary-crossing knowledge rather than revealed, relationally given, Source-initiated knowledge.

[21] John Walton, the Old Testament scholar, has argued that the knowledge of good and evil in Genesis should be understood as a reference to wisdom in its broadest sense, the capacity to make discerning judgments about all of reality. His work on the ancient Near Eastern context of Genesis is valuable here: in the world of Israel's neighbors, kings and gods were the proper possessors of this comprehensive wisdom. For a human being to claim it independently was an act of usurpation, a reaching for a domain that belonged to the divine. Walton's argument supports the reading this book is built on: the prohibition was not about protecting a secret. It was about protecting a relationship.

[22] The Boundary Between Creator and Creation

[23] Every boundary in Scripture exists for a reason, and the reason is almost always relational before it is legal. The tree of the knowledge of good and evil served as the boundary between the Creator and creation. This is the central claim of this chapter, and it is the frame through which every subsequent chapter will be read.

[24] On one side of the boundary stands God: eternal, omniscient, the uncreated Source of all knowledge that is true. He is the one of whom Solomon said, in his prayer at the dedication of the Temple, thou, even thou only, knowest the hearts of all the children of men (1 Kings 8:39, KJV). Not partially. Not approximately. He knows every human heart. Every secret. Every motive. Every wound. Every longing. Every question that a person has never found the words to ask. He knows.

[25] He is also the one of whom Jeremiah recorded this declaration: Do not I fill heaven and earth? saith the LORD (Jeremiah 23:24, KJV). This is not a God who is distant. This is not a God who created the world and then retreated to some elevated position from which He observes events with detachment. He fills heaven and earth. He is present in every corner of what He made. The boundary He drew in the garden was not drawn from a distance. It was drawn by the One who was already there, on both sides, sustaining the very existence of the tree He was forbidding.

[26] On the other side of the boundary stands creation, and within creation, the crown of it: humanity. Made in the image of God, which the theological tradition calls the imago Dei. Created with a capacity no other creature in the Genesis account possesses: the capacity to receive direct communication from the Creator. The animals were spoken into existence. The plants were spoken into existence. But Adam was formed personally, with the breath of the Creator entering him directly (Genesis 2:7). And before the prohibition was given, before any command was issued, before any boundary was named, there was conversation. Relationship. The walking of God in the garden in the cool of the day (Genesis 3:8), not as an inspection but as a communion.

[27] The boundary, then, was not drawn between a harsh God and a restricted people. It was drawn within a relationship. It was the relational architecture of an arrangement in which God would be the Source, and humanity would be the receiver. The tree was the marker that said: there is a way of knowing that belongs to Me. And I will give you everything you need. But this way, the way of independently acquired, self-generated, boundary-crossing knowledge, this way is not for you.

[28] But of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, thou shalt not eat of it: for in the day that thou eatest thereof thou shalt surely die.

[29] Genesis 2:17 (KJV)

[30] The command in Genesis 2:17 has been read for centuries primarily as a test of obedience. And it is that. But to read it only as a test of obedience is to miss what it reveals about the nature of the knowledge being forbidden, and about the One who was forbidding it.

[31] The phrase good and evil in the Hebrew tradition is a figure of speech that scholars call a merism. A merism uses two opposites to represent the totality of a category. When the psalmist says God knows our sitting down and our rising up (Psalm 139:2), he does not mean God only knows those two moments. He means God knows everything in between. In the same way, good and evil is not a moral binary. It is a comprehensive claim. To know good and evil is to know everything. The full range of moral and existential reality. The complete framework by which life is to be understood and judged.

[32] This is what the tree offered: total knowledge. Comprehensive wisdom. The capacity to define for oneself what is right and what is wrong, what is permitted and what is forbidden, what is true and what is false. And this is precisely the knowledge that God intended to give His children Himself. Not withheld. Not hidden. Given. But given through the relationship, through the walk in the garden, through the ongoing conversation between the Creator and the image-bearers He had placed in the world to steward it.

[33] The prohibition was therefore not the restriction of knowledge. It was the protection of the channel through which knowledge was meant to flow. God was saying, in effect: I am the Source. Everything you need to know about good and evil, about right and wrong, about how to live in the world I made for you, I will give you. But you will receive it from Me. That is the arrangement. And crossing this boundary would mean choosing a different source. A lesser source. A created source. A synthetic source that could approximate wisdom but could never produce it.

[34] Eternal Knowledge and Synthetic Knowledge

[35] The distinction between these two kinds of knowledge is the theological engine that drives this entire book. It is not a distinction that appears with this precise vocabulary in the biblical text. But it is a distinction that the biblical text makes on every page, in every covenant, in every prophetic warning, in every moment where God says, in one form or another: ask me. Receive from me. Do not go to the other source.

[36] Eternal knowledge is the knowledge that flows from the Source. It is the knowledge that God possesses inherently, that exists outside of time, that was never acquired because it was never not there. When God speaks into a human life, He speaks from the position of the One who knows the end from the beginning, who declares things not yet done (Isaiah 46:10). He does not inform based on what He has observed. He reveals based on what He is. This is the knowledge that the prophets received when they spoke words that made no earthly sense in their moment. This is the knowledge that Peter received when he said, Thou art the Christ, the Son of the living God (Matthew 16:16, KJV), and Jesus immediately identified the source: flesh and blood hath not revealed it unto thee, but my Father which is in heaven (Matthew 16:17, KJV). That is eternal knowledge. That is the Source speaking directly into a human heart.

[37] Synthetic knowledge is the knowledge that comes from crossing the boundary. It is created knowledge. Knowledge that humanity generates through observation, through experience, through the accumulation of what finite minds can gather from a fallen world. It is not without value. It is not entirely false. Science and philosophy and human wisdom have all produced genuine insights. But synthetic knowledge carries a fundamental limitation: it is bounded by the finitude of the knower. It can observe but it cannot see. It can reason but it cannot reveal. And when it is substituted for eternal knowledge, when it becomes the primary source rather than a secondary tool, it leads exactly where Proverbs said it leads: to the way that seems right, and ends in death.

[38] The Apostle Paul confronted this same distinction when he wrote to the church in Corinth: Where is the wise man? Where is the scribe? Where is the debater of this age? Has not God made foolish the wisdom of the world? (1 Corinthians 1:20, ESV). The wisdom of the world, the synthetic knowledge generated by the best human minds operating without the Source, is not neutral. It is the tree's fruit being redistributed in new forms. And Paul's argument is not that human wisdom is worthless but that it is insufficient. It cannot do what eternal knowledge can do. It cannot save. It cannot reveal the deep things of God. It cannot tell a person who they are, where they are going, or what their life is actually for.

[39] Andrew Murray, the nineteenth century devotional writer, wrote with characteristic directness about what he called the life of abiding in Christ: the soul that abides in Christ receives from Him a constant supply of grace and strength and knowledge that the soul who lives by its own resources can never generate. Murray was not using the language of this book. But he was describing the same distinction. The soul that receives from the Source and the soul that generates from itself are living in different epistemological worlds, even if they are sitting in the same pew.

[40] This distinction did not disappear when humanity left the garden. It has been the defining tension of every era of human history since. And the prophet Daniel, writing from Babylon, gave it its most forward-looking expression. He recorded a word that has found its fullest expression in the world we now inhabit: But you, Daniel, keep the prophecy a secret; seal up the book until the time of the end, when many will rush here and there, and knowledge will increase (Daniel 12:4, NLT). Many will rush here and there. Knowledge will increase. This is not a description of a world drawing closer to the eternal Source. It is a description of a world accelerating toward more and more sophisticated forms of synthetic knowledge, while the boundary between Creator and creation becomes increasingly obscured by the noise.

[41] Daniel also recorded the verse that follows, and it is just as important: Many will be purified, cleansed, and refined by these trials, but the wicked will continue in their wickedness, and none of them will understand. Only those who are wise will know what it means (Daniel 12:10, NLT). The same acceleration of knowledge. Two different responses. Those who are wise will know what it means. And the wisdom that allows a person to understand what is happening, to discern the times, to navigate the flood of synthetic knowledge without losing their orientation toward the Source, that wisdom does not come from rushing here and there. It comes from the same place it has always come from: the God who knows every human heart and fills heaven and earth.

[42] Why This Question Changes Everything

[43] Kierkegaard's challenge circles back here, as it always does. To be objective toward oneself is to ask a question that most people spend enormous energy avoiding: where am I actually getting my knowledge? Not in theory. Not in the answer I would give in a church setting if someone asked me how I make decisions. But in practice, in the actual moment when I am navigating something difficult, when I am forming an opinion about a person or a situation, when I am deciding what is right and what is wrong in a circumstance that the Bible does not directly address. Where is the knowledge coming from?

[44] This is not a question with an obvious or comfortable answer for most people. Most of us, if we are honest, are drawing from a mixture of sources at any given moment. Some revelation. Some tradition. Some personal experience. Some cultural assumption. Some fear. Some desire. The question is not whether we ever draw from the right source. The question is whether we are oriented toward the Source as the primary place from which we expect to receive.

[45] The tree is not gone. This is the other thing that must be said clearly from the opening chapter of this book. The tree of the knowledge of good and evil was not destroyed when Adam and Eve were expelled from the garden. It was replanted in human civilization in every generation since. It appears as philosophy when philosophy becomes the arbiter of truth. It appears as science when science oversteps its genuine domain and begins to pronounce on questions it was never designed to answer. It appears as political ideology when ideology promises a comprehensive understanding of what is wrong with the world and what will fix it. It appears as social media when the algorithm becomes the source of what is true, what is good, and what is worth caring about.

[46] And it appears, with remarkable frequency, inside the church. When tradition replaces the voice. When systematic theology replaces living revelation. When a person knows a great deal about God and has not heard from God in years. When zeal without knowledge, as Paul described it in Romans 10:2, drives sincere people to act from the best of intentions in ways that the Source never authorized. The tree is everywhere. And the boundary is still there. And the Source is still speaking.

[47] This is why the question matters. Not as an academic exercise. Not as a theological curiosity. But as the organizing question of a human life. The person who asks it honestly, and who is willing to keep asking it, is a person whose life is being reoriented toward the Source. And the person whose life is oriented toward the Source is the person Daniel described at the end of his vision: the one who, in the midst of a world where knowledge is increasing and many are rushing here and there, has the wisdom to know what it means.

[48] Everything that follows in this book is an attempt to trace that orientation across the full sweep of Scripture, from the garden to the resurrection, and to show that the question buried in Genesis 2:17 is not a historical question about what went wrong once. It is the permanent question of human life: where are you getting your knowledge? And the answer that God has been giving, in every covenant and every prophet and every apostle and ultimately in His own Son, has never changed. Come to Me. I am the Source. I know every heart. I fill heaven and earth. And I have never stopped speaking.

[49] Recurring Thread: Enoch  Genesis 5:22-24 records the entirety of what Scripture tells us about Enoch's life in three sentences: Enoch walked with God after he fathered Methuselah 300 years and had other sons and daughters. Thus all the days of Enoch were 365 years. Enoch walked with God, and he was not, for God took him (ESV). What is remarkable about this account is not its length but its simplicity. There is no record of Enoch's theology. There is no catalogue of his achievements. There is no list of the battles he fought or the nations he influenced. There is only this: he walked with God. In a world that had already begun its long drift away from the Source, in a genealogy full of men who lived and died, Enoch lived differently. He oriented himself toward the One who knows every heart and fills heaven and earth. He received rather than generated. He walked rather than rushed. And God took him. Enoch will appear again in these pages, because his life is a quiet answer to the question this chapter asks. He is the portrait of what it looks like to live from the eternal Source rather than the synthetic one. And that portrait is going to become more and more important as this book unfolds.

[50] ANTIDOTE

[51] Theological Application

[52] The theological application of this chapter is both a question and a reorientation. The question is the one Kierkegaard pointed toward and that the garden made unavoidable: where are you getting your knowledge? Not the knowledge you claim in principle. The knowledge you actually live from in the ordinary moments of an ordinary day. The boundary God placed between the Creator and creation in Eden was not a punishment waiting to be triggered. It was the outline of a love. The design of a Father who intended to be the Source of everything His children needed to understand about life, about good, about evil, and about themselves. The antidote to synthetic knowledge is not more information, not better reasoning, not deeper theological study, though none of those things are wrong. The antidote is a return to the posture of receiving. It is the decision, repeated in every moment of every day, to turn toward the Source before turning anywhere else. To be, as Kierkegaard suggested, objective enough about yourself to recognize when you have been eating from the wrong tree. And then to do what Enoch did: walk back toward the One who planted it, who forbade it, and who has been waiting on the other side of the boundary ever since, ready to give you everything you actually need.
